Excellent! You've mastered Modals. Now we move to one of the most common sources of errors in English `Subject-Verb Agreement` (also called Subject-Verb Concord). This is the rule that ensures your subjects and verbs "match" in number (singular or plural).

**Without agreement**: She walk to school. (Incorrect)

**With agreement**: She walks to school. (Correct)

This is a foundational skill that affects every sentence you speak or write. Master this, and you'll sound significantly more polished and professional.

---

# Topic 21: Subject-Verb Agreement

##  1. Concept Explanation

Subject-Verb Agreement means that the **subject** and the **verb** in a sentence must agree in number (**singular** or **plural**).

#### Singular Subject → Singular Verb (usually ends in -s in the present tense)

> He runs. / The cat is sleeping.

#### Plural Subject → Plural Verb (usually does NOT end in -s)
> They run. / The cats are sleeping.


#### Difficult words explained:

* **Agreement/Concord** = Matching, harmony, going together.

* **Number** = Singular (one) or Plural (more than one).

#### The 80/20 Rule for Subject-Verb Agreement:

80% of the time, it's simple: add **-s** to the verb for he/she/it/singular; use the base verb for I/you/we/they/plural. The remaining 20% contains tricky **cases** collective nouns, indefinite pronouns, "or/nor," and "there is/there are." Focus on these tricky cases to avoid 80% of your mistakes.

---

## 2. Rules (and Exceptions)

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1. Singular subjects** | Singular subjects take singular verbs (usually **+-s** in present). | **The dog barks. She works.** |
| **2. Plural subjects** | Plural subjects take plural verbs (no **-s** in present). | **The dogs bark. They work.** |
| **3. Compound subjects with "and"** | Usually plural (two or more things together). | **John and Mary are coming.** |
| **4. Compound subjects with "or/nor"** | The verb agrees with the **closest subject.** | **Either the boy or the girls are going. Either the girls or the boy is going.** |
| **5. Indefinite pronouns (*everyone, someone, anyone, nobody, etc.*)** | ALWAYS singular. | **Everyone is here. Someone has taken my pen.** |
| **6. Collective nouns (*team, family, group, committee*)** | Usually singular in American English (acting as one unit). | **The team is playing well.** |
| **7. "Each" and "Every"** | ALWAYS singular (even before plural nouns). | **Each of the students has a book. Every one is ready.** |
| **8. Uncountable nouns** | ALWAYS singular. | **The water is cold. The news is good.** |
| **9. "There is" / "There are"** | The verb agrees with the **noun that follows.** | **There is a book. There are many books.** |
| **10. Titles and names** | Singular, even if they look plural. | **"The Chronicles of Narnia" is a great series.** |


---

**Important Exceptions:**

* **"The police"** is plural. (*The police **are** investigating.*)

* **"People"** is plural. (*People **are** friendly here.*)

* Some nouns look plural but are singular: **mathematics, physics, news, statistics** (when referring to the subject). (*Mathematics **is** difficult.*)

* Some nouns look singular but are plural: **trousers, clothes, scissors, glasses** (eyewear). (*The scissors **are** sharp.*)

---

## 3. Structure Formula

There is no single formula, but the core rule is:

> [Subject] + [Verb (matches subject in number)] + [Object/Complement]

* _He_ (**S, singular**) + _runs_ (**V, singular**) + **fast**.
* _They_ (**S, plural**) + _run_ (**V, plural**) + **fast**.


---

## 4. Examples

#### Positive Sentences

1. **She** (S) **works** (V) hard. (Singular)

2. **They** (S) **work** (V) hard. (Plural)

3. **Everyone** (S) **is** (V) invited. (Indefinite pronoun &rarr; singular)

4. **The team** (S) **is** (V) winning. (Collective noun &rarr; singular in US English)

5. **The books** (S) **are** (V) on the table. (Plural)

#### Negative Sentences

1. **She does not work** hard.

2. **They do not work** hard.

3. **Everyone is not** invited. / **Not everyone is** invited.

4. **The team is not** winning.

5. **The books are not** on the table.

#### Interrogative Sentences (Questions)

1. **Does** she **work** hard?

2. **Do** they **work** hard?

3. **Is** everyone invited?

4. **Is** the team winning?

5. **Are** the books on the table?

#### Negative Interrogative Sentences

1. **Doesn't** she **work** hard?

2. **Don't** they **work** hard?

3. **Isn't** everyone invited?

4. **Isn't** the team winning?

5. **Aren't** the books on the table?

## 5. Real-Life Conversation

**Setting:** Two colleagues discussing a project team.

| Speaker | Dialogue |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Anna:** | "The team **is** working on the new project. **Everyone is** excited." |
| **Ben:** | "That's great! **Are** all the members **working** together?" |
| **Anna:** | "Yes, but there **are** some challenges. The data **is** incomplete." |
| **Ben:** | "**Does** anyone **have** the missing information?" |
| **Anna:** | "John and Mary **have** some data. Either John or Mary **has** (closest subject = Mary) the rest." |
| **Ben:** | "Good. Mathematics **is** not my strength, so I'll trust them!" |

## 6. Common Mistakes

| Common Mistake | Why it is wrong | Correct Version |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| She **work** hard. | Singular subject 'she' needs singular verb 'works'. | She **works** hard. |
| They **does** not know. | Plural subject 'they' needs plural verb 'do'. | They **do** not know. |
| Everyone **are** happy. | Indefinite pronoun 'everyone' is singular. | Everyone **is** happy. |
| The team **are** playing well. | In American English, collective nouns are singular. | The team **is** playing well. (US) / The team **are** playing well. (UK, acceptable) |
| There **is** many books. | 'Books' is plural &rarr; use 'are'. | There **are** many books. |
| Either John or Mary **are** coming. | With 'or', verb agrees with the **closest subject** (Mary = singular). | Either John or Mary **is** coming. |

## 7. Vocabulary (10 Tricky Nouns for Agreement)

| Noun | Singular or Plural? | Example Sentence |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1. News** | Singular (uncountable) | The news **is** good today. |
| **2. Mathematics** | Singular (subject/field) | Mathematics **is** my favorite subject. |
| **3. Physics** | Singular (subject/field) | Physics **is** challenging. |
| **4. Police** | Plural (usually) | The police **are** investigating the case. |
| **5. People** | Plural | People **are** generally kind. |
| **6. Trousers** | Plural (pair of ...) | These trousers **are** too tight. |
| **7. Scissors** | Plural (pair of ...) | The scissors **are** on the table. |
| **8. Family** | Singular (as a unit) / Plural (as individuals) | My family **is** large. (US) My family **are** all different. (UK) |
| **9. Team** | Singular (as a unit) / Plural (as individuals) | The team **is** winning. (US) |
| **10. Bread** | Singular (uncountable) | The bread **is** fresh. |

2. **Talk about the news.** Say: "The news on TV **is** [adjective]."

3. **Correct yourself aloud.** Say: "Everyone **are** here" (wrong), then "Everyone **is** here" (correct).

4. **Describe what people do.** Say: "People in my country **are** [adjective]."

5. **Give instructions using 'there is/there are'.** Say: "There **is** a book on the table. There **are** pens in the drawer."

| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Singular (He/She/It)** | Singular (+s/es) | She **runs**. |
| **Plural (They/We/You)** | Plural (base form) | They **run**. |
| **Indefinite (Everyone, Someone, Nobody)** | Singular | Everyone **is**. |
